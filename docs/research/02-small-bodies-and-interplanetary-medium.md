# Small Bodies & the Interplanetary Medium — Research Reference

> Research paper #02 for the Stellar 3D portfolio. Scope: dwarf planets, the
> asteroid belt, the Kuiper belt, Trojans / Centaurs / comets, the heliosphere,
> the Oort cloud, and the interplanetary dust (zodiacal light) + Local
> Interstellar Cloud. Every value below is web-verified against NASA/JPL, the
> Minor Planet Center, ESA, or peer-reviewed literature (cited inline), **not**
> recalled from memory. Uncertainties are carried where the source gives them.
>
> Prime directive for this section of the portfolio: **the belts are almost
> empty.** At true scale the asteroid belt and Kuiper belt are not dense dusty
> rings — they are vast, dark, mostly-vacuum volumes where individual bodies are
> hundreds of thousands to millions of kilometres apart. The current config
> renders them as "dense dusty donuts," which is the single biggest scientific
> inaccuracy in the small-body layer (see §4).

---

## 1. Executive summary

**Dwarf planets.** Nine bodies are covered (the IAU's five recognised —
Ceres, Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake — plus four strong candidates —
Gonggong, Quaoar, Sedna, Orcus). Key corrections the portfolio should absorb:
**Eris is the most massive dwarf planet but is very slightly *smaller* in
diameter than Pluto** (2,326 km vs 2,377 km), and it is **nearly white**
(albedo 0.96) because methane frost condenses uniformly over it and buries any
red tholins. Pluto is the reddish one (tholins + the nitrogen-ice heart,
Sputnik Planitia). Haumea is a genuine triaxial egg (2,122 × 1,688 × 1,036 km),
spins in 3.9 h, and has a ring. Sedna is one of the reddest objects known
(tholin sludge, nearly as red as Mars). Orcus is the neutral-grey "anti-Pluto."

**Asteroid belt.** It spans ~2.1–3.3 AU and holds only **≈2.39 × 10²¹ kg —
about 3% of the Moon's mass**, ~62% of which is in just four bodies (Ceres,
Vesta, Pallas, Hygiea; Ceres alone is 39%). The average distance *between*
belt asteroids is on the order of **~1 million km** — you could fly through it
and never see one. Kirkwood gaps at the 4:1 (2.06 AU), 3:1 (2.50 AU),
5:2 (2.82 AU), 7:3 (2.96 AU), and 2:1 (3.27 AU) Jupiter resonances carve the
distribution. Taxonomy: **C-type ~75% (dark, carbonaceous, albedo ~0.03–0.10),
S-type ~17% (reddish silicate, ~0.10–0.22), M-type (metallic, ~0.10–0.30)**,
with Vesta as the bright V-type (basaltic, albedo 0.42).

**Kuiper belt.** Classical belt 39.5–~48 AU, ending at the **Kuiper Cliff near
the 1:2 resonance (~47.7–48 AU)**. Split into a dynamically **cold** population
(low i, low e, at 42–45 AU, **ultra-red**, ~0.03 M⊕) and a **hot** population
(high i/e, redistributed from nearer Neptune, **neutral-to-red**, ~1% M⊕),
plus resonant **Plutinos (3:2, ~39.4 AU)** and **Twotinos (1:2, ~47.7 AU)** and
the scattered disc beyond. The defining visual fact is the **bimodal colour
split** — ultra-red tholin-coated cold classicals vs. neutral-grey hot objects.

**Comets, Trojans, Centaurs.** Comet nuclei are **among the darkest objects in
the Solar System** (albedo ~0.03–0.04, blacker than coal). Two tails: a
**blue ion/gas tail** (ionised CO⁺ fluorescence, pointed dead anti-sunward by
the solar wind) and a **yellow-white dust tail** (reflected sunlight, curved,
lagging the orbit). Halley: 74–76 yr, e = 0.967, retrograde i = 162°,
next perihelion **28 Jul 2061**. Jupiter Trojans (~1 million >1 km) sit 60°
ahead (L4) and behind (L5) Jupiter at ~5.2 AU, dark and reddish. Centaurs
(5.5–30 AU) are unstable comet/asteroid hybrids, colour-bimodal (very-red vs
blue-grey).

**Heliosphere & beyond.** Solar wind 300–800 km/s; termination shock crossed by
Voyager 1 at **~94 AU (2004)**; heliopause crossed by **Voyager 1 at 121 AU
(25 Aug 2012)** and **Voyager 2 at 119 AU (5 Nov 2018)**. No true bow shock —
a gentler "bow wave." The **Oort cloud** (~2,000–100,000+ AU, up to ~0.03–3.2
ly) is a spherical shell (outer) around a denser disc-like Hills cloud (inner),
the source of long-period comets. The Sun currently sits inside the **Local
Interstellar Cloud** ("Local Fluff," ~30 ly, ~7,000 K, ~0.3 atoms/cm³).
**Zodiacal light** is sunlight scattered off interplanetary dust concentrated
along the ecliptic — faint, pale, sun-coloured.

## 3. Deep dives

### 3.1 Dwarf planets — the colour logic

The single most important corrective for the portfolio: **redness is a
tholin story, and whiteness is a frost story.** Irradiation of surface
methane/nitrogen ices by UV and cosmic rays builds up reddish-brown organic
"tholins." A body reads **red** when that tholin crust is exposed and stable
(Sedna, Gonggong, Makemake, Arrokoth, the cold classicals). A body reads
**white/pale** when a fresh volatile frost keeps re-condensing on top and
covers the tholins:

- **Eris (`#dad6cd`, albedo 0.96)** — the archetype. Because Eris is currently
  near aphelion (~96 AU) and frigid, methane frost condenses uniformly and
  "would cover up any deposits of red tholins," giving an *almost white and
  homogeneous* surface. This is the opposite of Pluto and is worth calling out
  in the fact card.
- **Pluto (`#c9b6a0`)** — mottled: charcoal-black tholin regions
  (Cthulhu Macula), dark orange, and the brilliant white nitrogen-ice heart
  **Sputnik Planitia** (convecting N₂ ice, >98% nitrogen with CH₄/CO traces).
  Overall coloration is closer to Io than to a uniform world.
- **Haumea (`#e3e0d9`)** — 66–80% *pure crystalline water ice*, hence bright
  white, with one localised dark-red organic-rich patch. It is a genuine
  triaxial ellipsoid (2,122 × 1,688 × 1,036 km) from its 3.9-hour spin, and it
  has a narrow ring (~2,287 km radius) — the first ring found at a TNO.
- **Sedna (`#7a3a2a`)** — among the reddest objects known, "nearly as red as
  Mars," from billions of years of tholin accumulation with no resurfacing.
- **Orcus (`#bcc6cc`)** — the neutral-grey outlier: crystalline water ice from
  past cryovolcanism, featureless visible spectrum. The genuine "anti-Pluto"
  claim is *orbital* — same 3:2 Neptune resonance and ~245 yr period as Pluto,
  but ~180° out of phase. (It is **not** the same *size* as Pluto — ~910–958 km
  vs Pluto's 2,377 km.)

### 3.2 Asteroid belt — the emptiness is the point

The belt's total mass (~3% of the Moon) is spread across 2.1–3.3 AU. With an
**average inter-asteroid distance of ~1 million km**, every spacecraft that has
crossed it (Pioneers, Voyagers, Galileo, Dawn, all the outer-planet missions)
has passed through without collision risk — targets have to be *deliberately
aimed for*. Any visualisation that shows a dense, rock-choked ring is
Hollywood, not science. The belt is structured by Jupiter: mean-motion
resonances clear the **Kirkwood gaps**, and the C→S→M gradient (dark
carbonaceous outer belt, brighter silicate inner belt) records a temperature
gradient in the early solar nebula. Vesta is special — the only intact rocky
protoplanet left, its basaltic (V-type) crust the source of the HED meteorites,
and the brightest asteroid in our sky (mag 5.1, occasionally naked-eye).

### 3.3 Kuiper belt & scattered disc — bimodal red vs neutral

The classical belt is the primordial, undisturbed heart (especially the *cold*
population near 44 AU — low inclination, low eccentricity, ultra-red, and never
scattered by Neptune). The *hot* population and the resonant/scattered
populations were stirred and transported outward during Neptune's migration,
and read more neutral. The belt effectively ends at the **Kuiper Cliff**
(~48 AU); why is still open (Neptune's migration history, or a distant unseen
perturber). For the portfolio, the two must-hit facts are (1) the belt is
**sparse** (its dynamically-hot mass is only ~1% of Earth, the cold only
~0.03%), and (2) its colour is **red-to-neutral**, driven by tholins — never
blue.

### 3.4 Comets — two tails, one very dark heart

A comet is a dark (albedo ~0.03–0.04) icy nucleus that grows a coma and tails
only near the Sun. The **ion tail is blue and ruler-straight, pointing exactly
away from the Sun** (solar-wind-driven CO⁺ fluorescence); the **dust tail is
yellow-white and curved**, lagging along the orbit because released grains
follow their own slightly different orbits. Occasionally a third **anti-tail**
appears to point *sunward* — a pure line-of-sight effect when Earth crosses the
comet's orbital plane. Halley is the canonical example: retrograde
(i = 162°), highly eccentric (0.967), a peanut-shaped 15 km coal-black nucleus,
returning in **2061**.

### 3.5 Trojans & Centaurs

**Jupiter Trojans** ride Jupiter's orbit (~5.2 AU) at the L4 ("Greek camp,"
60° ahead) and L5 ("Trojan camp," 60° behind) Lagrange points — ~1 million
bodies >1 km, rivalling the main belt in number. They are **dark and reddish**
(albedo ~0.03–0.10; largest is 624 Hektor, ~200 km), likely captured
outer-system planetesimals (Nice model). NASA's *Lucy* is touring them.
**Centaurs** are the transitional icy bodies between Jupiter and Neptune
(~5.5–30 AU) on dynamically short-lived orbits (a few Myr). They are
comet/asteroid hybrids and **colour-bimodal (very-red vs blue-grey)** — e.g.
5145 Pholus (very red), 10199 Chariklo (250 km, *has rings*), and **2060 Chiron**
(the first-discovered centaur, showing both a cometary coma *and* a ring).

### 3.6 Heliosphere, Oort cloud, and the interplanetary medium

The solar wind inflates the heliosphere; it slows abruptly at the
**termination shock (~94 AU, V1 2004)**, becomes the turbulent heliosheath, and
gives way to the interstellar medium at the **heliopause (~120 AU)**. Both
Voyagers have crossed it (V1 2012 @121 AU; V2 2018 @119 AU) and are now in
interstellar space, though still well inside the Oort cloud. The **Oort cloud**
(spherical outer shell to ~100,000+ AU, disc-like inner Hills cloud) is the
reservoir of long-period comets and marks the Sun's true gravitational edge —
approaching a light-year, roughly a quarter of the way to Proxima. Filling the
inner system, **interplanetary dust** (from comet shedding and asteroid
collisions) concentrates along the ecliptic and scatters sunlight into the
faint, pale, sun-coloured **zodiacal light** (and the anti-solar gegenschein).
The whole heliosphere is currently plowing through the **Local Interstellar
Cloud**, a warm (~7,000 K), tenuous (~0.3 atoms/cm³) wisp ~30 ly across.

### 4.1 High priority (scientific accuracy)

1. **Belts rendered as "dense dusty donuts" — the opposite of reality.**
   `destinations.js:433-439` (`BACKGROUND_BELTS`) and its comment
   ("*so they read as the dense dusty donuts the reference imagery shows*").
   The asteroid belt is **~3% of the Moon's mass with ~1 million km between
   bodies**; the Kuiper belt is comparably sparse. At the portfolio's true
   1:1 AU scale they should read as **almost empty** — a faint scatter of
   isolated specks, not a solid ring. This is the top correction. See §5.

2. **Kuiper belt colour is blue — should be red/neutral.**
   `destinations.js:438` — `kuiper … color: "#9fb0d0"` (pale blue-grey).
   Kuiper objects are **red-to-neutral (bimodal), never blue**. Recommend a
   warm neutral or muted red-brown, e.g. `#8a7360` (neutral-hot) with a redder
   accent `#9a6a52` for a cold-classical sub-band. (Blue would only be defensible
   as pure artistic license, but it contradicts the "scientific accuracy is the
   prime goal" constraint.)

3. **Asteroid-belt colour is too bright/warm.**
   `destinations.js:437` — `asteroid … color: "#c9b48a"` (light tan). The belt
   is **~75% dark C-type (albedo ~0.06)**; its integrated colour is a dark,
   desaturated grey-brown, e.g. `#6b6157` or `#7a6f5f`. The bright tan reads as
   an S-type-dominated belt, which it is not.

4. **Vesta mislabelled S-type — it is V-type (basaltic).**
   `dwarfPlanets.js:31` — "*second-largest belt body (S-type)*". Vesta is the
   prototype **V-type**, basaltic, albedo **0.42** (the *brightest* asteroid).
   Fix the parenthetical to "(V-type, basaltic)". (Its `#b8a98c` colour is
   acceptable as a bright neutral, though it could be a touch brighter/greyer.)

### 4.2 Medium priority (facts & proportions)

5. **Orcus "same size class as Pluto" is wrong.**
   `dwarfPlanets.js:78` — "*same size class and orbital period as Pluto*".
   Orcus is ~910–958 km vs Pluto's 2,377 km (~40%). The **orbital-period /
   anti-phase** claim is correct; the size claim is not. Reword to "same
   orbital period as Pluto but ~180° out of phase" and drop "size class."

6. **Dwarf-planet relative sizes invert the true ordering.**
   `dwarfPlanets.js` render radii: Eris 0.057, Gonggong/Makemake 0.046,
   Haumea 0.044, Quaoar 0.041, Orcus/Hygiea 0.038, Sedna 0.05 — all **larger**
   than Pluto's 0.034 (`destinations.js:349`). In reality **every one of these
   is smaller than Pluto except Eris (≈equal)**. This is a deliberate
   visibility floor (documented in the header comment), but if size-ordering
   fidelity is wanted, scale radii to true relative diameters above the floor
   (order: Pluto ≈ Eris > Makemake ≈ Gonggong > Haumea(mean) ≈ Sedna ≈ Orcus ≈
   Ceres > Quaoar). At minimum, **Eris should not out-size Pluto** on screen.

7. **Scattered-disc/detached bodies placed too close in.**
   `dwarfPlanets.js` positions remap Eris (`45.6…`), Gonggong (`53…`) and
   Sedna (`59…`) into roughly the 42–68 AU band. True mean distances: **Eris
   a = 67.7 AU (now ~96 AU), Gonggong a = 66.9 AU, Sedna perihelion 76 AU /
   a = 506 AU.** They currently sit in/near the classical belt rather than the
   remote scattered disc, undated the sense of their remoteness. If schematic
   placement is acceptable, at least push Eris and Sedna visibly *beyond* the
   Kuiper band.

8. **"First active interstellar comet" contradicts the Borisov entry.**
   `objects.js:173` calls 3I/ATLAS "*the first ACTIVE interstellar comet*",
   but `objects.js:178` correctly describes 2I/Borisov (2019) as an active
   interstellar *comet*. Borisov was the first active interstellar comet;
   'Oumuamua was inactive. Reword ATLAS to "the third interstellar object and
   an active comet" (or "the most clearly active"), not "first."

### 4.3 Low priority (polish / nice-to-have)

9. **Bennu "sugars."** `dwarfPlanets.js:18` — the 2023–2025 sample analyses
   confirmed amino acids, all five nucleobases, ammonia and brines; "sugars"
   specifically is not a headline confirmed result for Bennu (it was for the
   Murchison meteorite). Consider "amino acids and nucleobases — building
   blocks of life" to stay strictly defensible.

10. **Ceres/Hygiea "most massive C-type" nuance.** `dwarfPlanets.js:39` calls
    Hygiea "*the most massive dark C-type carbonaceous asteroid*." Ceres is also
    C/G-type carbonaceous and far more massive; the claim only holds if Ceres
    is excluded as a dwarf planet. Fine as written if you consider Ceres a
    dwarf planet, but worth a mental asterisk.

11. **Things that are already correct — keep them.** Pallas (B-type, i≈35°,
    bluish-grey `#9aa4ac`) ✓; Eris pale `#dad6cd` + "highly reflective" ✓;
    Haumea egg + 4 h + ring + `scale`/`#e3e0d9` ✓; Sedna 11,400 yr + deep red
    `#7a3a2a` ✓; Quaoar ring-beyond-Roche + Weywot ✓; Gonggong deep-red +
    Xiangliu + 550 yr ✓; Chiron first-centaur + coma + ring, placed between
    Saturn and Uranus ✓; **Halley** (`objects.js:162`) ✓ — 76 yr, blue ion tail
    anti-sunward, curved dust tail, 1986/2061 all accurate; belt spans
    `2.1–3.3 AU` and `30–50 AU` ✓; Ceres @ 2.77 AU / Pluto @ 39.48 AU ✓.

## 5. Implementation recommendations

### 5.1 Make the belts read as sparse/empty (top priority)

At the true 1:1 AU scale (`AU_UNIT = 4274`), the asteroid belt occupies
2.1–3.3 AU → ~9,000–14,000 scene units, and the Kuiper belt 30–50 AU →
~128,000–214,000 units. Across those enormous volumes the real object count is
tiny relative to the volume. Recommendations:

- **Drop particle density dramatically** and let the background go dark between
  specks. The belts should be *suggested*, not *filled*. If the current
  `BACKGROUND_BELTS` tori use a dense particle field or a semi-opaque disc,
  reduce count/opacity until the dominant impression is "mostly empty space
  with occasional glints."
- **Rewrite the `destinations.js:434-436` comment** — it currently instructs the
  opposite ("dense dusty donuts… so they read as dense"). Replace with the
  scientific reality: the belts are ~3% of a Moon-mass (asteroid) / sparse
  (Kuiper), ~1 million km between bodies; render as a faint, sparse scatter.
- **Vary particle brightness/size by a power law** (many tiny/dark, a few
  bright) to echo the real size–frequency distribution rather than a uniform
  haze.
- Optionally **hint the Kirkwood gaps** as faint radial density dips at 2.50,
  2.82, 2.96 AU (3:1, 5:2, 7:3) for the science-literate viewer.

### 5.2 Fix belt colours

- `destinations.js:437` asteroid `#c9b48a` → **`#6f645a`** (dark C-type-weighted
  grey-brown). If you want warmth, keep it desaturated.
- `destinations.js:438` kuiper `#9fb0d0` (blue) → **`#8a7360`** neutral warm,
  or split into a redder cold-classical inner band `#9a6a52` and a neutral hot
  outer `#7d7166` to nod to the bimodal colour split.

### 5.3 Dwarf-planet fact/colour touch-ups

- `dwarfPlanets.js:31` — Vesta "(S-type)" → **"(V-type, basaltic)"**; it is the
  *brightest* asteroid, so a slightly brighter neutral is defensible.
- `dwarfPlanets.js:78` — Orcus: drop "same size class," keep the anti-phase
  orbit claim.
- Consider adding **Eris's whiteness reason** to its fact card
  (`dwarfPlanets.js:48`): "*so cold that methane frost re-condenses over the
  whole surface, burying red tholins — hence nearly white, unlike reddish
  Pluto.*" This is a genuinely striking, portfolio-worthy contrast.
- If size ordering is tuned (§4.2 #6), ensure **Eris ≤ Pluto** on screen and,
  ideally, Quaoar/Orcus render smaller than Makemake/Gonggong.

### 5.4 Position realism for the far objects (optional)

If schematic placement can bend, move **Eris and Sedna** visibly beyond the
Kuiper band (they are scattered-disc/detached, currently 60–96 AU out) so the
scene conveys their remoteness. This is lower priority than the belt-density and
colour fixes, since the whole layout is compressed-then-remapped by design.

### 5.5 Comet & interstellar wording

- `objects.js:173` — soften 3I/ATLAS "first ACTIVE interstellar comet" to avoid
  contradicting the (correct) 2I/Borisov description at `objects.js:178`.
- The Halley entry is a model to emulate — its two-tail description (straight
  blue ion tail anti-sunward + curved dust tail) is exactly right and could be
  the template for any future comet fact cards.
